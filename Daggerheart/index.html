1. What It Is

Collaborative fantasy, not tactical bureaucracy

Story Shape Heartfelt and epic

Adventures swing between intimate character beats, big emotional decisions, and dangerous set-piece conflicts.

Rules Philosophy Rulings over rules

The fiction decides what makes sense first; the mechanics resolve uncertainty without taking over the table.

Combat Feel Fast, fluid, cinematic

No rigid initiative order or strict action economy. Spotlight and consequences keep battles moving.

Winning Tell something memorable

Failure changes the story instead of dead-ending it. Even losses become fuel for the next turn of the campaign.

Daggerheart is a collaborative fantasy roleplaying game of incredible magic and heroic adventure, built to tell stories that are both emotionally grounded and spectacularly mythic.

2. Table Culture

What players are expected to do

Be a fan of your character

Show off what they do best, but also let their flaws and messy choices matter. Growth comes from pressure.

Spotlight your friends

Set people up. Ask for help. Hand scenes across the table. Daggerheart works best when everyone gets turns to shine.

Build the world together

Players are not passive passengers. You help establish details, relationships, stakes, and the emotional shape of the campaign.

Golden rule

Make the game your own. The core book explicitly frames the rules as tools for the table, not a cage. If a ruling better serves the fiction and your group agrees, the game expects you to use it.

Player principles from the core book+
  • Be a fan of your character and their journey.
  • Spotlight your friends.
  • Address the characters and address the players.
  • Build the world together.
  • Play to find out what happens.
  • Hold on gently and leave room for the fiction to evolve.
Safety and social contract+

The rulebook is explicit that the table should be safe, respectful, and adjustable. Session zero, content boundaries, and safety tools are not optional polish. They are part of how the game expects a group to start well.

3. World and Tone

Big myth, flexible setting, personal stakes

The Mortal Realm

The main stage for adventure: land, sea, sky, factions, mortals, and the consequences of divine history.

The Hallows Above

Seat of the New Gods and divine territories, bound to sacrifice, influence, and distant intervention.

The Circles Below

Lower realms of banished power, corruption, violence, and the lingering shadow of the Divine War.

How to read the setting

The core realms establish a shared mythic grammar for ancestries, demons, deities, magic, and danger, but the book also encourages reflavoring and modification. Your table can use the core assumptions, a campaign frame, or a custom world built together.

4. Character Creation

Build a concept, then give it teeth

1

Start with session zero and a character concept

Come in with a simple premise first: a lost captain, a noble fugitive, a witch with dangerous obligations. Let the campaign tone shape the final version.

2

Choose your class and subclass

Your class defines your role, starting Evasion and Hit Points, core feature, Hope feature, and your two domains.

3

Choose ancestry and community

Ancestry shapes lineage and physicality. Community shapes what kind of place or culture formed you. Both grant meaningful features.

4

Pick domains, cards, gear, and experiences

Domain cards express the magical or martial themes your class uses. Gear and experiences make your version of that class specific.

5

Answer background and connection questions

Relationships are not afterthoughts. The game pushes you to define why this party matters to one another before the first crisis lands.

Character creation is not only about picking a combat role. It is about deciding how your character looks at the world, what they want, what they fear, and how they fit into the party’s emotional geometry.

4A. First Character Runway

Use this order if you want to build a playable character without overthinking it

Fast build checklist

  1. Write a one-line concept that sounds fun in scenes, not just in combat.
  2. Choose the class whose spotlight feels right: protector, schemer, hunter, miracle-worker, or force of nature.
  3. Use the class domains to confirm tone. If the domains do not match your fantasy, switch classes now.
  4. Pick ancestry and community that explain where your character learned to survive.
  5. Choose gear, cards, and experiences that make your version of the class specific rather than generic.
  6. Finish with party ties: who you trust, who worries you, and why this group matters.

Best shortcut for new players

Start from class fantasy first, then use domains and campaign frame to sharpen the edges. Daggerheart rewards emotional clarity more than build math.

6. How Play Works

Scenes, spotlight, and the duality engine

A

The GM frames the situation

You are given details, tension, environment, and pressure points. Players ask questions and add detail.

B

The spotlight lands on someone

Who acts next is driven by the fiction, not a strict turn queue. If it makes sense, you act.

C

If the outcome matters, roll action dice

Pick the appropriate trait, apply modifiers, roll the two d12 Duality Dice, and see both success and emotional momentum.

D

The story changes

Every roll should move the fiction. Nothing happens is not the intended default state of the game.

Rolling with Hope

If the Hope die is higher, you roll with Hope. On action rolls, you gain Hope even if you fail. Hope fuels helping allies, activating class Hope features, using experiences, and initiating tag-team moments.

Rolling with Fear

If the Fear die is higher, the GM gains Fear and the scene complicates. You may still succeed, but the cost, danger, or collateral changes the shape of the moment.

The four-step action roll loop+
  • Pick the trait that best fits the move and set or learn the Difficulty.
  • Add modifiers, experiences, help, and any advantage or disadvantage dice.
  • Roll both d12 Duality Dice and total everything.
  • Resolve both outcome and momentum: success or failure, plus Hope or Fear.
Why this feels different from traditional initiative+

Daggerheart intentionally avoids rigid rounds. That means players can chain fiction together, react more naturally, and hand off scenes in ways that feel more like dramatic momentum than bookkeeping.

7. Combat and Survival

How damage, pressure, and danger actually land

Evasion

Your Evasion is the Difficulty enemies roll against. It can look like nimble dodging, timing, footing, shields, or magical warding.

Hit Points and thresholds

Damage is compared against your thresholds. Minor marks 1 HP, Major marks 2 HP, and Severe marks 3 HP.

Stress

Stress tracks strain, panic, overexertion, and magical or emotional pressure. Filling it makes you Vulnerable.

Damage cadenceRoll proficiency dice

Successful attacks roll damage dice based on weapon or effect, often scaled by Proficiency.

Critical hitsStart from max dice

Critical attack rolls deal the maximum possible result of the damage dice, then add a normal damage roll on top.

DowntimeRecovery matters

Downtime is how you clear pressure, regain position, and make the campaign breathe between dangerous scenes.

DeathStory-facing, not casual

Marking your last Hit Point pushes you into the game's death procedure rather than instantly erasing you.

Story is consequence

The rulebook is explicit: every roll changes the scene. A failed lockpick roll is not just “the door stays shut.” It becomes guards, alarm wards, time pressure, or a forced new approach.

8. Growth and Campaigns

How characters level and what kinds of campaigns the game supports

Tiered advancement

Levels 1-10 are split into escalating tiers, with stronger subclass features, more cards, better thresholds, and sharper identities over time.

Level choices matter

Advancement is not a single-track ladder. You choose among more Hit Points, more Stress, more Evasion, more Proficiency, more cards, or subclass growth.

Campaign frames

The book includes distinct campaign structures that tune tone, assumptions, and worldbuilding from the start.

The Witherwild

Haunted wilds

Nature, decay, magic, and danger in an untamed frame that leans into primal mystery.

Shadowed fantasy
