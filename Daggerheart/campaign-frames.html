Campaign frames are the quickest way to decide what kind of fantasy story your table is actually telling. They tune mood, pressure, and world assumptions before the first scene ever begins. This page turns the core frame lineup into a player-facing guide: what each frame suggests, what kind of party thrives there, and how to choose the one that matches your group’s appetite.

1. Choosing a Frame

Pick the pressure you want the world to apply

If you want travel and myth

Choose a frame with terrain as antagonist

The Witherwild and Colossus of the Drylands both imply a world where the land itself shapes the campaign’s drama.

If you want loyalty tests

Choose a frame built on factions or corruption

Five Banners Burning and The Age of Umbra suggest stories where allegiance, secrecy, and compromise have real weight.

If you want a strong tonal hook

Choose the frame with the clearest image in your head

Beast Feast and Motherboard are especially powerful when your table wants a bolder, stranger identity from the start.

Best practice

At session zero, ask not only “What sounds cool?” but “What kind of complications do we want every few sessions?” The best frame is the one whose problems you are excited to keep seeing.

2. Frame Atlas

Six ways to point the campaign at a very different fantasy

The Witherwild

Haunted wilds

A frame for tables that want nature to feel ancient, beautiful, and dangerous. Expect mystery, decay, primal magic, and a sense that the land remembers more than the people crossing it.

  • Best for Rangers, Druids, Guardians, and anyone tied to place or omen.
  • Strong if your group likes exploration with eerie emotional texture.
  • Questions it invites: What is rotting, and who is trying to survive it?

Five Banners Burning

Conflict and factions

A frame built for divided loyalties, clashing powers, and people asked to choose who they are when kingdoms, banners, or causes demand an answer.

  • Best for parties who enjoy politics, military pressure, and faction play.
  • Excellent for Seraphs, Guardians, Bards, and Rogues with strong convictions.
  • Questions it invites: What do you defend, and what will it cost?

Beast Feast

Monsters and excess

A frame that reads loud, strange, and hungry. It suggests monstrous spectacle, oversized personalities, and fantasy that is willing to be a little dangerous and a little deliciously absurd.

  • Best for tables that like expressive characters and memorable set pieces.
  • Pairs well with bold Bards, Sorcerers, Warriors, and flamboyant Rogues.
  • Questions it invites: What appetite is driving the world out of balance?

The Age of Umbra

Shadowed fantasy

A frame of creeping darkness, hidden powers, and a world shaped by threat that does not always announce itself cleanly. It supports secrecy, suspicion, and slow-burn dread.

  • Best for groups who enjoy secrets, moral ambiguity, and uneasy alliances.
  • Pairs especially well with Rogues, Wizards, Sorcerers, and duty-bound Seraphs.
  • Questions it invites: What is already lost, and what shadow is still spreading?

Motherboard

Genre-bending

A frame that signals Daggerheart can move beyond straightforward fantasy assumptions. It is ideal for tables that want strange premises, surprising metaphors, and a more experimental flavor of worldbuilding.

  • Best for groups comfortable co-authoring a weird world together.
  • Works beautifully with Wizards, Bards, Sorcerers, and inventive Guardians.
  • Questions it invites: What rules of reality are different here, and who benefits?

Colossus of the Drylands

Epic traversal

A frame that suggests scale: vast spaces, immense obstacles, and a campaign built around movement through environments that feel too large to ignore. Travel becomes mythic action.

  • Best for parties who want journey, hardship, and awe as recurring pillars.
  • Pairs well with Rangers, Druids, Warriors, and any character built for endurance.
  • Questions it invites: What do you keep moving toward, and what does the world demand in return?

3. Session Zero Prompts

Questions that help a frame lock into place

Pressure

What kind of trouble shows up often?

Choose whether your campaign repeats war, wilderness, corruption, hunger, mystery, or impossible scale. Repetition is identity.

Promise

What emotional color should victories have?

Triumphant, bittersweet, defiant, eerie, or ecstatic. Naming this early helps every class and backstory click harder.

Party role

Why is this group necessary?

Are you scouts, rebels, escorts, survivors, pilgrims, or opportunists? The answer determines how the frame hits back.

4. Party Fit

Use these broad pairings if you want a fast recommendation

For heroic defense stories

Five Banners Burning or The Age of Umbra

These frames reward characters with convictions, secrets, and the ability to hold the line when everything gets complicated.

For primal or travel parties

The Witherwild or Colossus of the Drylands

Take these when your group wants terrain, survival, movement, and wonder to matter almost as much as direct conflict.

For louder, stranger campaigns

Beast Feast or Motherboard

Pick these if your table loves strong aesthetic identity, unusual premises, and memorable character energy over grounded default fantasy.
