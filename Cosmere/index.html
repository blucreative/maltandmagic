Primer overview

What the Cosmere primer should do for players

This page is built to help a new player understand the setting at a glance, then move into character creation with fewer blind spots. It is not just lore; it is a bridge from the broad Cosmere to the specific character you are bringing to the table.

1. Set the lens

Pick the kind of Cosmere story you want to play: high-stakes heroism, court intrigue, survival against larger powers, or a personal story shaped by hidden forces.

2. Choose the world

Decide which world your character belongs to or begins in. That choice shapes the tone, the culture, and the kind of power expressions that make sense for the table.

3. Pick the pressure

Every good Cosmere character has a force pushing back on them: obligation, exile, a broken promise, a dangerous gift, or a secret they cannot keep forever.

4. Land the hook

Build a starting hook that the table can use immediately. A clear hook helps the GM frame scenes and makes the character feel ready on session one.

Orientation

First-visit guidance for new players

The orientation fires on first visit so the page can explain itself without making the player hunt for instructions. It walks through the order that makes the most sense: understand the setting, choose the starting world, then build the character around that decision.

The orientation is intentionally short. It should tell a new player what matters, what can wait, and where the character creation guidance lives.

Step one: understand the setting

The Cosmere is a connected fantasy universe, but every world still has its own culture, stakes, and expression of magic. Start with the world the campaign is actually using.

Step two: choose the character role

Figure out what the character does in scenes before deciding how they do it. Protector, explorer, investigator, negotiator, mystic, or something stranger all work.

Step three: define the source of power

Power in the Cosmere is never just a number on a sheet. It comes from a world, a bond, a discipline, or a dangerous relationship with Investiture.

Step four: lock the starting hook

Finish with a reason the rest of the table cares about this person right now. A clean hook keeps the first session moving.

Character creation

How to build a Cosmere character without getting lost

The easiest way to build here is to work from story outward. Start with where the character comes from, then what they can do, then what drives them, and finally what trouble follows them into play.

1. Concept

Who are they before the power matters?

Occupation, upbringing, and the problem they keep running into. This anchors the character so they feel real even in a high-fantasy setting.

2. World

Which world shaped them?

Choose the world or region that explains their customs, language, and starting assumptions about power.

3. Expression

How does their power show up?

Make the power feel like an expression of the character, not just a combat trick. The best choices also explain a social cost or narrative consequence.

4. Role

What do they do for the party?

Pick the kind of scenes they should shine in: protecting people, solving mysteries, moving quietly, speaking for the group, or unlocking a strange problem.

5. Bond

Who matters enough to complicate everything?

A mentor, rival, family tie, oath, or debt gives the GM an easy way to pull the character into the story.

6. Hook

What starts the adventure now?

One unresolved event is enough. The best hooks are immediate, personal, and easy for the table to understand.

Character worksheet

Answer these before you pick details

  • What world are you from, and what does that world expect of you?
  • What kind of power, training, or strange edge do you bring?
  • What problem follows you that the rest of the party will notice?
  • What do you want badly enough to keep moving?
If you are new to the setting

Start with a world example, then reskin the details to fit the table. Roshar, Scadrial, and Nalthis are useful reference points for tone even if your campaign is doing something broader or more original.

World prompts

Use a world-specific frame to guide the build

Roshar-style play

Big storms, strong bonds, and a sense that oaths and ideals matter. Good for characters who feel pulled between duty and identity.

Scadrial-style play

Metal, motion, ingenuity, and social pressure. Good for sharp problem-solvers, crew dynamics, and characters who rely on cleverness as much as power.

Nalthis-style play

Color, breath, ritual, and strange beauty. Good for characters whose power feels artistic, symbolic, or deeply tied to life force and presentation.
