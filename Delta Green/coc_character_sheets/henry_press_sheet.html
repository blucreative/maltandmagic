Personal Description
Big, broad, once-handsome in a way that still shows. Carries extra weight around the middle that wasn't there two years ago. Dresses like someone who used to have somewhere to be. Dark eyes that watch rooms too carefully.
Ideology & Beliefs
Used to believe intelligence and hard work meant something — that the world was basically fair. That broke completely. Now quietly convinced there's something wrong underneath things he can't quite name. The occult reading isn't faith, it's picking at a scab. He'd be embarrassed if anyone knew.
Significant People
His father, Gerald Press — quiet, undemanding, loves Henry in the way that men of that generation do, by not asking questions and making sure there's food in the house. Henry missed his mother's decline while chasing something in London. That sits on top of everything else. No real friends. The people he grew up with are still here but he left them behind without a backward glance, and now nobody quite knows what to say.
Meaningful Locations
The library — quiet, free, his main refuge. Times his visits for the quiet periods. The pub occasionally with Gerald, the most normal Henry feels. His dad's sitting room in the evenings, watching television without talking much. Not unhappy. Just very small.
Treasured Possessions
A silver pocket watch — his mother's gift on his 18th birthday, the last birthday before the calls became less frequent. Carries it every day. An old laptop from the London years, cracked casing, takes four minutes to boot. He maintains it obsessively. Fixing things he's already broken.
Traits
Has a habit of being the most competent person in a room and resenting that it's visible. Physically restless — always doing something with his hands. Pen clicking, pocket watch turning, tearing the corner off a beer mat. Gerald has learned to find it comforting.
Injuries & Scars
None physical. The damage is internal and ongoing — a low hum of shame and self-recrimination he's learned to function around. He doesn't sleep well. He hasn't for two years.
The Margaret Bond
About three months after he came back, Henry was in the library with nowhere better to be. Margaret was returning books. She didn't ask where he'd been or why he was back. She handed him something she thought he'd like, walked him to the desk and sorted him out a library card. No ceremony, no questions. He's read forty-odd books since then. It's the kindest thing anyone has done for him in years and he's never said so.
