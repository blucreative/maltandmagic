# Name Register Meaning & etymology
1
Auren
OR-en
 Formal
The opening day. Temple rites, shrine dedications, the week's first prayers.
From aur — dawn-gold. Shares its root with aurora; in Auroran culture this is deliberate and unspoken.
In use: "First thing Auren." "We leave on Auren."
2
Brem
BREM
 Common
A short, blunt working day — the week's first full labour day, nothing ceremonial about it.
From brem — a grinding sound, the sound of millstones. Old craft-guild slang that became the day's name.
In use: "Brem already and the order's not done." "Back by Brem."
3
Thaelon
THAY-lon
 Formal
A longer, older name — feels like it belongs to a different era. Used in formal records and legal dating.
From thael (thread) + on (forward) — the threading of time onward. Aeonius's fingerprint, never acknowledged.
In use: "Dated the third Thaelon of Corendar." Rarely used in speech — people say "third day."
4
Keld
KELD
 Common
The week's midpoint approach — market prep, travel day, the day people move between towns.
From kel — a ford, a crossing. The day you cross toward the middle of the week.
In use: "I'll be on the road Keld." "Keld prices are always worse" (market prep markup).
5
Narven
NAR-ven
 Common
Market day. The week's social and commercial centre. Debts settled, deals made, news exchanged.
From narn (midpoint, exchange) + ven (meeting, gathering). Retains the familiar Narn root from the existing calendar.
In use: "See you Narven." "Narven prices." "Wait until Narven and ask around."
6
Glaer
GLAIR
 Common
The week turns. Work continues but the second half has begun — a subtle shift in folk mood.
From glae — a glint, a turning of light. Nymue's faint fingerprint — the day the moon begins its count toward the next phase in old lunar reckoning.
In use: "Past Glaer already." "Glaer and I'm still not done."
7
Solveth
SOL-veth
 Formal
The easing day — not a rest day, but the pace lightens. Apprentices given shorter hours in many trades.
From sol (sun, warmth) + veth (breath, ease). Solariel's subtle mark — the day named for the sun's pause before it begins its descent toward dusk.
In use: "Solveth hours" (shorter working day). "At least it's Solveth."
8
Pelk
PELK
 Common
The push day — the week's hardest working day by folk custom, the last full labour day before rest approaches.
From pelk — a shove, a final effort. Purely working-class in origin, no cosmic etymology. The day has no dignity and everyone knows it.
In use: "Pelk again." "Don't schedule anything important on Pelk." "A real Pelk of a day."
9
Seradel
ser-AH-del
 Formal
The settling day — accounts closed, work finished, preparation for rest. Formal and slightly solemn in character.
From ser (settling, closing) + adel (toward the hearth). Charon's quiet fingerprint — the day you settle what is owed before the week closes.
In use: "Seradel accounts" (closing the books). "Pay by Seradel." Rarely shortened.
10
Kaelden
KAY-den
 Formal
Rest day. Hearth and home. The week's close. Formally observed — most trades closed, travel discouraged.
From kael (completion, wholeness) + den (hearth, rest). Solves the Degeth/Daudeth collision entirely. No pantheon echo — rest belongs to no god specifically, which is itself a theological position.
In use: "Kaelden, finally." "Kaelden hours" (nothing's open). "See you next Auren."
